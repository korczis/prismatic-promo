Trust

Problem Gateway

"Your partners, suppliers, and acquisition targets present polished surfaces -- and you need to see what is underneath."

The Problem

Every significant business relationship involves trust -- and every trust decision should be informed by evidence. You are acquiring a company. You are onboarding a critical supplier. You are evaluating a technology partner. You are assessing a potential investment. In each case, the counterparty presents their best face: curated references, favorable financials, clean compliance records, professional websites.

The question is always the same: what are you not seeing? Are the financial statements audited or self-reported? Are the "independent" references actually independent? Is the technology platform actually built on the architecture they describe, or is it a thin layer over legacy systems? Are there undisclosed legal proceedings, beneficial ownership structures, or sanction exposure?

Traditional due diligence addresses these questions through document review, management interviews, and targeted verification. But document review trusts the documents. Management interviews trust the management. And targeted verification only targets what you think to look for.

Why Typical Approaches Fail

Self-reported information is inherently biased. Financial statements prepared for a transaction are designed to present the most favorable picture within accounting rules. Management presentations highlight strengths and contextualize weaknesses. Data rooms are curated, not comprehensive.

Reference checks confirm the obvious. No counterparty provides references who will say negative things. Reference checks validate that the company exists and has satisfied customers. They do not surface hidden risks, undisclosed relationships, or structural vulnerabilities.

Point-in-time due diligence captures a snapshot. A pre-acquisition due diligence report reflects the company's state during the assessment period. After closing, the acquired entity evolves -- key personnel leave, customers churn, technical debt surfaces -- and the acquirer discovers risks that did not exist during diligence or were not visible at that depth.

Siloed verification misses cross-domain signals. Legal due diligence, financial due diligence, and technical due diligence are typically conducted by separate teams with separate methodologies. Signals that span domains -- a beneficial owner who appears in sanctions databases, financial patterns that suggest undisclosed technology debt, corporate structures that minimize tax liability at the cost of operational resilience -- fall between the silos.

How Prismatic Addresses It

Prismatic addresses counterparty trust through multi-domain intelligence fusion that combines public records, OSINT, technical assessment, and epistemic verification into unified entity profiles.

Multi-Source Entity Resolution. The platform resolves entity identity across 250+ sources, cross-referencing corporate registries, beneficial ownership databases, sanctions lists, court records, domain registrations, SSL certificates, and technology fingerprints. A single entity query produces a unified profile that connects disparate data points.

Counterparty Risk Scoring. Security ratings (A-F grades, 300-900 scores) quantify external risk posture based on observable technical indicators: SSL configuration, email security, DNS hygiene, exposed services, vulnerability exposure. These scores provide an independent, evidence-based risk signal that does not rely on self-reported information.

Beneficial Ownership Mapping. The platform traverses corporate ownership chains through registry data, mapping ultimate beneficial owners and identifying structures that may indicate opacity risks -- layered offshore entities, circular ownership, nominee directors.

Cross-Domain Signal Correlation. Intelligence from legal, financial, technical, and operational domains is fused through the NABLA framework's signal correlation pipeline. Contradictions between domains -- a company claiming SOC2 compliance while exposing unpatched services -- are preserved and surfaced rather than averaged away.

Continuous Monitoring. Unlike point-in-time assessments, the platform enables continuous counterparty monitoring. Post-acquisition, post-onboarding, or throughout a business relationship, changes in the counterparty's risk profile -- new legal proceedings, domain changes, certificate expirations, ownership transfers -- trigger alerts.

Outputs and Artifacts

  • Unified entity profiles combining data from 250+ sources with per-attribute confidence scores
  • Counterparty risk scorecards with A-F grades and numeric scores based on observable evidence
  • Beneficial ownership chain maps showing corporate structures and ultimate beneficial owners
  • Cross-domain contradiction reports surfacing inconsistencies between self-reported and observed data
  • Continuous monitoring dashboards tracking counterparty risk evolution over time
  • Due diligence evidence packages with source provenance and independence assessment for every finding

Limits and Boundaries

  • Entity resolution accuracy depends on data availability in the relevant jurisdictions. Entities in jurisdictions with limited public registry data produce lower-confidence profiles.
  • Beneficial ownership mapping relies on publicly accessible registry data. Jurisdictions with opaque ownership disclosure requirements limit visibility into ultimate beneficial ownership.
  • Security ratings measure externally observable technical posture. They do not assess internal security controls, organizational security culture, or non-technical risk factors.
  • Continuous monitoring detects publicly observable changes. Private events -- board decisions, undisclosed transactions, internal policy changes -- are outside the monitoring boundary.
  • The platform provides intelligence products, not legal opinions. Findings should inform but not replace qualified legal counsel for transactional decision-making.

Claims / Evidence / Assumptions / Limits

Claims

  • Multi-source entity profiles provide more complete counterparty visibility than document-based due diligence alone.
  • Cross-domain signal correlation surfaces contradictions that siloed assessments miss.
  • Continuous monitoring detects counterparty risk changes that point-in-time assessments cannot.

Evidence

  • 250+ OSINT source adapters provide multi-jurisdictional entity data.
  • Security rating system produces evidence-based risk scores on A-F / 300-900 scales.
  • NABLA framework enforces source independence tracking and contradiction preservation.

Assumptions

  • Counterparty risk is better assessed through observable evidence than through self-reported documentation.
  • Cross-domain intelligence fusion produces insights that domain-specific analysis misses.

Limits

  • Data availability varies significantly across jurisdictions and entity types.
  • Observation-based assessment cannot substitute for insider access to non-public information.
  • Intelligence products inform decisions but do not replace professional judgment.
