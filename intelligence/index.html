Intelligence Fusion: Multi-Spectrum Signal Convergence

The Prismatic Platform implements a fundamentally different approach to intelligence gathering and analysis. Rather than treating intelligence as a linear pipeline where raw data flows through sequential transformation stages, the platform operates as a convergent intelligence fusion reactor -- a system where signals from 250+ sources are simultaneously ingested, correlated, validated against epistemic axioms, and synthesized into actionable knowledge through a network of 434 AIAD agents.

This is not simple data aggregation. Intelligence Fusion requires that every signal be evaluated for provenance, temporal relevance, source independence, and contradiction potential before it enters the platform's knowledge graph. The NABLA Infinity framework enforces seven non-negotiable axioms that prevent the system from accepting unvalidated claims, discarding contradictory evidence, or converging prematurely on conclusions that lack sufficient epistemic support.

The result is an intelligence platform that knows what it knows, knows what it does not know, and -- critically -- preserves the distinction between the two. Every intelligence product carries confidence scores validated through the Trinity Gate, ensuring that structural consistency, logical coherence, and formal necessity are all satisfied before a claim is established.

The Intelligence Fusion Architecture

The platform's intelligence architecture operates across five convergence layers, each responsible for a distinct phase of the fusion process:

Layer 5: Environmental Grounding (Gen 19)    -- External reality validation
Layer 4: Entity Resolution & Graph Analysis   -- Knowledge graph construction
Layer 3: Intelligence Synthesis               -- Multi-source signal fusion
Layer 2: Source Processing & Extraction       -- Pattern-based data extraction
Layer 1: Signal Acquisition                   -- Raw data collection from 250+ sources

Each layer operates independently but communicates through the platform's PubSub event system, allowing real-time propagation of intelligence updates across all active processing pipelines. The telemetry infrastructure monitors every signal transition, providing complete observability into the fusion process.

Mycelial Intelligence: Knowledge Propagation Across the Agent Network

The platform's intelligence distribution system is modeled on biological mycelial networks -- the underground fungal networks that enable trees in a forest to share nutrients and chemical signals. In the Prismatic Platform, Mycelial Intelligence refers to the mechanism by which knowledge propagates across the network of 434 AIAD agents without requiring centralized coordination.

When any agent in the network acquires new intelligence -- whether through OSINT collection, entity resolution, or environmental grounding -- that intelligence is not stored in isolation. Instead, it is published as a mycelial signal that propagates through the agent network based on relevance scoring. Agents subscribe to signal categories aligned with their specialization, and the mycelial router ensures that each signal reaches every agent that could benefit from it while avoiding noise flooding.

Mycelial Propagation Properties

The mycelial network exhibits four critical properties that distinguish it from conventional message-passing systems:

Selective Propagation: Signals do not broadcast uniformly. Each agent maintains a relevance profile that determines which signals it receives. A Czech Business Intelligence Specialist receives signals about Czech legal entity changes but not about network infrastructure vulnerabilities. A Hawkeye Security Auditor receives visitor behavioral anomaly signals but not M&A financial intelligence.

Contradiction Amplification: When the mycelial network detects that two agents hold contradictory beliefs about the same entity or relationship, it does not resolve the contradiction by choosing one. Instead, it amplifies the contradiction signal, ensuring that both perspectives are preserved and flagged for higher-level synthesis. This directly implements the Addiction Preservation doctrine -- the platform's commitment to never smoothing over inconvenient truths.

Temporal Decay Awareness: Every signal in the mycelial network carries a temporal stamp and a decay function. Intelligence about a company's registered address from three years ago is weighted differently than the same information from yesterday. The network automatically reduces confidence in aging signals while preserving their provenance chain for historical analysis.

Emergent Pattern Detection: Because signals propagate through the network rather than being stored in a central repository, the network itself becomes a pattern detector. When multiple independent agents report signals that individually seem unremarkable but collectively suggest a pattern -- such as a series of related company registrations across different jurisdictions -- the mycelial network surfaces the emergent pattern through convergence detection.

Mycelial Integration Example

defmodule PrismaticAgents.Mycelial.IntelligenceRouter do
  @moduledoc """
  Routes intelligence signals through the mycelial network
  based on agent relevance profiles and signal categories.
  """

  use GenServer

  alias PrismaticAgents.Registry
  alias PrismaticStorage.GraphAdapter

  @type signal :: %{
    source: atom(),
    category: atom(),
    payload: map(),
    confidence: float(),
    provenance: list(),
    timestamp: DateTime.t()
  }

  @spec propagate(signal()) :: {:ok, non_neg_integer()} | {:error, term()}
  def propagate(signal) do
    with {:ok, validated} <- validate_provenance(signal),
         {:ok, enriched} <- apply_temporal_decay(validated),
         {:ok, targets} <- resolve_target_agents(enriched),
         {:ok, count} <- deliver_to_agents(enriched, targets) do
      emit_telemetry(:signal_propagated, %{
        source: signal.source,
        category: signal.category,
        target_count: count,
        confidence: enriched.confidence
      })
      {:ok, count}
    end
  end

  defp resolve_target_agents(signal) do
    agents =
      Registry.list_agents()
      |> Enum.filter(&relevance_match?(&1, signal))
      |> Enum.sort_by(&relevance_score(&1, signal), :desc)

    {:ok, agents}
  end
end

defmodule PrismaticOsintCore.Patterns.CzechAres do
  @moduledoc """
  Extraction pattern for Czech ARES business registry.
  Maps ARES XML/JSON responses to platform entity format.
  """

  @behaviour PrismaticOsintCore.ExtractionPattern

  @impl true
  def source_id, do: :czech_ares

  @impl true
  def extract(raw_response) do
    with {:ok, parsed} <- parse_response(raw_response),
         {:ok, entities} <- map_entities(parsed),
         {:ok, enriched} <- attach_provenance(entities) do
      {:ok, enriched}
    end
  end

  defp map_entities(parsed) do
    entities =
      parsed
      |> Enum.map(fn record ->
        %PrismaticOsintCore.Entity{
          type: :company,
          name: record["obchodni_firma"],
          identifiers: %{ico: record["ico"], dic: record["dic"]},
          jurisdiction: :cz,
          registered_address: extract_address(record),
          legal_form: record["pravni_forma"],
          status: map_status(record["stav_subjektu"]),
          confidence: 0.95,
          provenance: [:czech_ares],
          extracted_at: DateTime.utc_now()
        }
      end)

    {:ok, entities}
  end
end

Entity Resolution and Graph Analysis

Raw intelligence signals become truly powerful only when they are resolved into a unified knowledge graph. The platform's Entity Resolution engine, backed by KuzuDB as the graph database, transforms millions of individual data points into a connected graph of entities and relationships.

The Resolution Challenge

Entity resolution in intelligence work is fundamentally harder than in conventional data engineering. The same person may appear as "Jan Novak" in the Czech business registry, "J. Novak" in a LinkedIn profile, "Ing. Jan Novak, MBA" in a court filing, and "novak.j@company.cz" in an email header. Each occurrence carries different metadata, different confidence levels, and potentially different temporal validity. The entity resolution engine must determine which of these references point to the same real-world entity while preserving the provenance of each individual observation.

Resolution Pipeline

The entity resolution pipeline operates in four phases:

Phase 1 -- Candidate Generation: For each incoming entity signal, the system generates a set of candidate matches from the existing knowledge graph using blocking keys (normalized name variants, identifier matches, address proximity).

Phase 2 -- Pairwise Comparison: Each candidate pair is scored across multiple dimensions: name similarity (Jaro-Winkler, phonetic encoding), identifier overlap (ICO, DIC, email domains), temporal proximity, and contextual signals (shared addresses, shared board memberships).

Phase 3 -- Cluster Decision: Pairwise scores are aggregated into cluster decisions using transitive closure with confidence thresholds. If entity A matches entity B with 0.92 confidence and entity B matches entity C with 0.88 confidence, the system evaluates whether A-C should also be linked, applying the Trinity Gate to prevent false positive chains.

Phase 4 -- Graph Integration: Resolved entities are merged into the KuzuDB knowledge graph, preserving all source observations as evidence nodes linked to the resolved entity. This allows any conclusion to be traced back to its constituent evidence.

Graph Query Interface

Once entities are resolved into the knowledge graph, the platform provides a powerful query interface for relationship discovery and pattern analysis:

defmodule PrismaticGraph.Intelligence do
  @moduledoc """
  High-level intelligence queries against the KuzuDB knowledge graph.
  Supports entity traversal, relationship discovery, and pattern matching.
  """

  alias PrismaticGraph.KuzuAdapter

  @spec find_connections(entity_id :: String.t(), depth :: pos_integer()) ::
          {:ok, list(map())} | {:error, term()}
  def find_connections(entity_id, depth \\ 3) do
    query = """
    MATCH path = (source:Entity {id: $id})-[*1..#{depth}]-(target:Entity)
    RETURN path, length(path) AS distance,
           [r IN relationships(path) | type(r)] AS relationship_types,
           target.name AS target_name,
           target.type AS target_type
    ORDER BY distance ASC
    LIMIT 100
    """

    KuzuAdapter.execute(query, %{id: entity_id})
  end

  @spec corporate_structure(ico :: String.t()) ::
          {:ok, map()} | {:error, term()}
  def corporate_structure(ico) do
    query = """
    MATCH (company:Company {ico: $ico})
    OPTIONAL MATCH (company)<-[:OWNS]-(owner:Entity)
    OPTIONAL MATCH (company)-[:HAS_BOARD_MEMBER]->(member:Person)
    OPTIONAL MATCH (company)-[:SUBSIDIARY_OF]->(parent:Company)
    RETURN company, collect(DISTINCT owner) AS owners,
           collect(DISTINCT member) AS board_members,
           collect(DISTINCT parent) AS parent_companies
    """

    KuzuAdapter.execute(query, %{ico: ico})
  end
end

Risk Scoring Model

Each visitor receives a composite risk score from 0 (benign) to 100 (critical threat), computed as a weighted combination of dimensional risk signals. The scoring model is calibrated through the platform's AI Drift monitoring to prevent score inflation or deflation over time.

defmodule PrismaticVisitorIntelligence.RiskScoring do
  @moduledoc """
  Composite risk scoring for visitor behavioral analysis.
  Produces scores from 0-100 with confidence intervals.
  """

  @dimension_weights %{
    navigation: 0.20,
    technical: 0.15,
    network: 0.25,
    temporal: 0.10,
    content: 0.20,
    interaction: 0.10
  }

  @spec compute_risk(visitor_profile :: map()) ::
          {:ok, %{score: float(), confidence: float(), factors: list()}}
  def compute_risk(visitor_profile) do
    dimensional_scores =
      @dimension_weights
      |> Enum.map(fn {dimension, weight} ->
        score = score_dimension(dimension, visitor_profile)
        {dimension, score * weight}
      end)

    composite_score =
      dimensional_scores
      |> Enum.map(&elem(&1, 1))
      |> Enum.sum()
      |> min(100.0)

    confidence = compute_confidence(visitor_profile)
    factors = extract_risk_factors(dimensional_scores)

    {:ok, %{score: composite_score, confidence: confidence, factors: factors}}
  end
end

Environmental Grounding Engine (Generation 19)

The most recent addition to the intelligence fusion architecture is the Environmental Grounding Engine, introduced in Generation 19 of the platform's autonomous evolution. Environmental Grounding addresses a fundamental limitation of intelligence systems: the tendency to build increasingly elaborate internal models that drift away from external reality.

The Grounding Problem

Intelligence fusion systems face a subtle but critical failure mode. As the knowledge graph grows and internal models become more sophisticated, the system can develop a self-reinforcing worldview that appears consistent internally but no longer reflects reality. A company might be modeled as financially healthy based on historical data even though it filed for insolvency last week. A network address might be classified as belonging to one organization when it was reassigned months ago.

Environmental Grounding solves this by establishing continuous bidirectional validation between the platform's internal knowledge graph and external reality anchors -- authoritative sources that represent ground truth for specific domains.

Grounding Anchors

The Environmental Grounding Engine maintains connections to external reality anchors organized by domain:

Legal Reality: Official government registries (ARES, Companies House, SEC EDGAR) that authoritatively define corporate existence and legal status.

Network Reality: DNS systems, BGP routing tables, and certificate transparency logs that define the current state of internet infrastructure.

Financial Reality: Stock exchanges, financial reporting databases, and credit rating agencies that reflect current financial status.
