Philosophy & Metaphysics -- Research Frameworks for Formal Logic, Thought Experiments, and Existential Risk Analysis

Theoretical foundations and research frameworks for applying multi-agent coordination, epistemic verification, and formal reasoning to metaphysics, philosophy of mind, decision theory, and existential risk modeling within the Prismatic Platform

Abstract

This document provides a comprehensive overview of the Prismatic Platform's philosophy and metaphysics research domain -- a collection of 25 theoretical frameworks designed to study the application of multi-agent systems, epistemic reasoning, and formal verification to classical and contemporary philosophical problems including personal identity, free will, decision theory paradoxes, meta-ethics, and existential risk. The domain spans five primary research areas: personal identity and persistence, decision theory and paradoxes, meta-ethics and value theory, philosophy of mind and consciousness, and existential and applied philosophy.

Each framework leverages the platform's agent orchestration infrastructure, contradiction preservation axioms, and Trinity Gate validation to computationally model philosophical thought experiments that have resisted resolution for centuries. The emphasis throughout is on making philosophical intuitions precise enough for formal analysis while preserving the genuine difficulty that makes these problems philosophically interesting.

Introduction

Context and Motivation

Philosophy has always been concerned with problems that resist easy formalization -- questions about identity, consciousness, value, and existence that seem to slip through the nets of any formal system powerful enough to capture them. Yet the history of philosophy is also a history of increasing formalization, from Aristotle's syllogistic through Frege's predicate logic to contemporary modal logic and formal epistemology. Computational philosophy extends this trajectory by using multi-agent simulation to explore the consequences of philosophical positions that are too complex for purely analytical treatment.

The Prismatic Platform's philosophy domain was conceived as a laboratory for operationalizing thought experiments. When Parfit asks what happens to personal identity in a brain-splitting scenario, or when Newcomb's problem poses a challenge to causal decision theory, these can be modeled as multi-agent scenarios where the philosophical positions become competing strategies whose consequences can be systematically explored. The platform's NABLA Infinity framework, with its commitment to signal plurality and contradiction preservation, is uniquely suited to philosophical inquiry where maintaining genuine disagreement is more valuable than forcing premature resolution.

Problem Definition

Computational philosophy research faces several interconnected theoretical challenges:

  1. Formalization Without Trivialization: Philosophical problems derive their interest from ambiguity and depth. Computational models risk stripping away precisely the features that make a problem philosophically significant. Frameworks must preserve philosophical nuance within formal structures.

  2. Paradox Representation: Many philosophical problems involve genuine paradoxes -- the Sorites heap, Newcomb's problem, the Ship of Theseus -- where consistent formal representation may be impossible. Models must support inconsistency-tolerant reasoning.

  3. Value Incommensurability: Ethical frameworks frequently encounter values that resist comparison on any single scale. Multi-agent models must represent genuine incommensurability rather than forcing commensuration.

  4. Consciousness and Qualia: Philosophy of mind raises questions about subjective experience that may be fundamentally resistant to third-person computational modeling. Frameworks must be honest about these limits.

  5. Existential Risk Assessment: Applied philosophy of existential risk requires integrating speculative reasoning about unprecedented scenarios with rigorous probability assessment -- a combination that strains standard epistemic frameworks.

The Ship of Theseus framework operationalizes the ancient paradox by modeling entities as agent-maintained composites with explicit persistence criteria. Different philosophical positions -- mereological essentialism, four-dimensionalism, relative identity theory -- become competing persistence functions whose divergent verdicts on identity can be systematically compared.

The Newcomb's problem framework is particularly notable for implementing competing decision theories as distinct agent strategies. Causal decision theorists two-box while evidential decision theorists one-box, and the framework enables systematic study of which strategy performs better across different predictor accuracy levels and iterated scenarios -- bringing empirical tractability to a debate that has been purely theoretical since 1969.

By implementing agents with different internal representations that produce identical behavioral outputs, the framework enables systematic study of whether functionalism can account for phenomenal consciousness -- or whether there is an explanatory gap that no functional account can bridge.

Future Research Directions

  1. Formal Phenomenology: Computational models of phenomenal structure using category theory and type theory to bridge the explanatory gap between physical and experiential descriptions
  2. AI Alignment Philosophy: Formal frameworks connecting philosophical value theory to machine learning alignment problems, including corrigibility, value loading, and goal stability
  3. Experimental Philosophy Integration: Combining multi-agent simulation with empirical data from experimental philosophy to calibrate computational models against human philosophical intuitions
  4. Paraconsistent Logic Systems: Implementing dialetheist and paraconsistent logics for philosophical domains where classical logic's explosion principle renders reasoning impossible
  5. Digital Ethics Frameworks: Philosophical analysis of digital personhood, virtual property rights, and the moral status of artificial agents
